<b>Glassware required : </b><br>
1)	Burette and Pipette <br>
2)	Conical flask (150 ml) <br>
3)	Measuring cylinder  <br>
4)	pH meter <br><br>

<b>Chemicals required : </b><br>
1)	NaOH solution (0.1 M)<br>
2)	HCl (0.25 N)<br>
3)	KCl solution (1 M)<br>
4)	Glycine (0.1 M)<br>
5)	Distilled water.<br><br>

<b>Laboratory procedure : </b><br>
1)	Take 50 mL of NaOH solution (0.1 M) in a 50 mL burette and adjust zero reading.<br>
2)	Pipette out 25 mL of the given solution of glycine in a 250 mL beaker and add 25 mL of distilled water to the amino acid solution using pipette.<br>
3)	Insert the cleaned pH electrode into the beaker solution and record the initial pH of the solution.<br>
4)	Do not remove the electrode from the beaker till the end of the experiment.<br> 
5)	Add NaOH in 0.5mL increment from the burette. Stir the solution and mix it well.<br>
6)	Record the corresponding pH values until the pH starts increasing drastically. At this time, add 0.1 mL increments of NaOH till the pH stabilizes around 8.<br>
7)	After reaching pH 8, continue adding 0.5 mL increments of NaOH till you reach pH 11.<br>
8)	Plot the graph of pH vs volume of NaOH solution.<br>
9)	The two almost horizontal parts of the graph give the values of pKa<sub>1</sub> and pKa<sub>2</sub> for glycine. Use mid-points of these regions to get the values.<br>
10)	The average of these values (pKa<sub>1</sub> and pKa<sub>2</sub>) gives the pI of glycine.<br><br>

<b>Precautions : </b><br>
1)	Handle the burette carefully.<br>
2)	Take the reading of 0.5 ml interval continuously.<br>
3)	pH-meter calibration must be done carefully.<br>
4)	Allow the pH meter reading to stabilize before recording each value to minimize errors in the titration curve.<br><br>
